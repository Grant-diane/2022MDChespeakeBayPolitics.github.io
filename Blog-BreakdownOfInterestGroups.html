"Breakdown of Local Environmental Interest Groups"

What Organizations Work to Improve the Bay? June 6th, 2022

Since humans have inhabited the region, the Chesapeake Bay has played an important role in human development. The livelihoods of generations have relied on the region to support industries such as fishing, shipbuilding, agriculture, and tourism. The industrial revolution brought much more development and urbanization to the region, especially as Baltimore boomed becoming a major shipping port and trade hub. With industrialization came chronic overfishing that led to severely depleted oyster, shad, and herring populations. Overfishing, industrial growth, and the overall impact of human use of the Bay began to take their toll causing severe pollution levels.

Noticing this in the context of the larger environmental movement, researchers began paying attention to the decline of the Chesapeake Bay ecosystem in the 1970s. Before academics took note, Maryland locals started noticing negative trends as population and business grew and wanted to get ahead of future problems due to continual development. The largest Chesapeake Bay interest group, The Chesapeake Bay Foundation (CBF), was founded around 1967 by involved citizens of Baltimore and the Eastern Shore. During its early years, the CBF launched several educational programs and worked with a Congressman to advocate for the EPA to conduct a seven-long year study of the Bay which laid much of the groundwork for the work that is done today. The full study of that proposal can be found here. The Chesapeake Bay Agreement of 1983 was a recognition of the need to organize efforts across government boundaries to address pollution in the Bay. The same year of its passing, the Chesapeake Bay Program (CBP) was established with similar goals to the Chesapeake Bay Foundation.

Since their founding, the CBP and CBF have both been involved in a number of highly successful agreements and policy pushes. Most recently the Chesapeake Watershed Agreement was updated in 2020 with the help of the CBP. In the past year the, CBF continued its efforts across the board, focusing on a court battle over the 2019 Affordable Clean Energy Rule, working with agricultural sectors to implement better regulation, and steadily continuing oyster restoration. There are countless local organizations all partnered with a common goal of protecting the natural resource of the bay. The CBP has developed a mapping tool that allows users to discover local groups near them involved with the issues (https://www.chesapeakebay.net/action/join).

Each of these groups contributes to a vast political network of actors working on behalf of the bay, its ecosystem, and all those who depend on it. One of the biggest roles of these organizations outside of educational outreach is advocacy and lobbying. One example of an upcoming bill in the Maryland House that further addresses bay issues is H.R.3540, the Chesapeake bay Science, Education, and Ecosystem Enhancement Act of 2021, otherwise known as SEEE. The act mainly seeks to reestablish the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration's (NOAA), Chesapeake Bay Office along with a handful of other actions including the creation of grants and programs to benefit Bay research and health. The bill has not made much headway right now only being introduced in May 2021 and recently in February being approved by the House Natural Resources Committee. The SEEE Act is, however, a great example of an upcoming piece of legislation that will likely receive support from one or more interest groups as the legislation moves through the pipeline. These interest groups are significant when attempting to pass legislation, generate funding, and publish research that aids in the long-term improvement of Chesapeake Bay health.
